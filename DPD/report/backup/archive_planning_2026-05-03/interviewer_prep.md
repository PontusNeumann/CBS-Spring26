# Interviewer Prep — FNZ Wealth-Platform Outsourcing Case

This file is the practical field guide for conducting the interviews if the teacher confirms the scope and theoretical framing. It is written for the interviewer, not for the final report. The internal logic remains aligned with `project_plan.md` and `interview_plan.md`.

---

## What Is Next

### If the teacher approves the scope

1. Schedule the three FNZ interviews in this order if possible: B, A, C.
2. Confirm confidentiality before recording or taking detailed notes.
3. Send each interviewee a short topic brief, not the full theory frame.
4. Conduct 30 to 45 minute semi-structured interviews.
5. Fill Appendix B in `paper_body_draft.md` immediately after each interview while memory is fresh.
6. Extract 3 to 5 usable observations per interview into the Results tables.
7. Write Discussion sections 5.1 to 5.3 from the interview evidence.
8. Decide whether the optional bank-side validation interview is needed after the three FNZ interviews.

### If the teacher asks for a narrower scope

Keep FNZ as the case, but reduce the paper to two primary lenses: transaction-cost economics and dynamic capabilities. Platform concentration can then become a shorter implication inside the discussion rather than a full analytical pillar.

### If the teacher challenges the confidential-interview strategy

Offer the safer version: three anonymized expert interviews used as qualitative primary material, with public FNZ mandates and corporate facts doing the factual grounding. Avoid implying that the interviews alone establish firm-level facts that should be publicly cited.

### Opening script

Thank you for taking the time. I am writing an individual CBS exam paper on FNZ and the shift in European wealth management toward outsourced platform infrastructure. The interview is confidential and will be anonymized. I am interested in mechanisms and examples, not client-sensitive details. If a question touches something confidential, please answer at a general or anonymized level.

### Interview style

- Ask for concrete examples before asking for interpretation.
- Avoid theory terms unless the interviewee uses them first.
- Do not ask the interviewee to validate the thesis directly.
- Push gently on trade-offs: benefits, tensions, failure points, and boundary conditions.
- Mark strong quotes immediately with `QUOTE`.
- Mark unclear or sensitive claims with `CHECK`.

### Evidence to capture every time

- A role/context statement that establishes source credibility.
- One concrete example.
- One trade-off or limitation.
- One forward-looking observation.
- One sentence that can support a theory-to-case analytical claim.

### Confidentiality reminder

Do not ask for named-client confidential details, revenue figures, internal pipeline information, non-public commercial terms, or client-specific operational failures. If a public case is mentioned, keep the question tied to what the public mandate reveals rather than what happened inside the deal.

### Questions And Conditional Follow-Ups

#### Opening

**Q1. Role and scope:** How would you describe your seat at FNZ today and what falls inside your remit?

- If the answer is broad: Which parts of the European market do you see most directly?
- If the answer is corporate: Could you describe the remit in terms of decisions or relationships you actually touch?

**Q2. Strategic positioning:** How would you characterize FNZ's position in European wealth management today, in your own words rather than the corporate one?

- If they emphasize scale: What does scale make possible that would be difficult for a smaller provider?
- If they emphasize technology: Is the strategic advantage mainly software, operations, ecosystem access, or the combination?
- If they mention banks' transformation needs: What has changed in banks' expectations over the last few years?

#### Block 1 — Network Effects, Multi-Homing, And Switching

**Q3. New-client effect:** When a new institutional client joins the platform, what changes, if anything, for the institutions already on it?

- If they say "nothing direct": Are there indirect benefits through regulatory updates, asset coverage, integrations, or operating learning?
- If they say "scale benefits": Where do those benefits show up first: cost, product coverage, reliability, compliance, or speed of change?
- If they mention standardization: What stays standardized, and what still needs client-specific adaptation?

**Q4. Core backbone versus edge vendors:** Banks often use multiple vendors around the edges. Why is the core wealth operating backbone different?

- If they emphasize complexity: Which parts of the complexity make parallel backbones unrealistic?
- If they emphasize governance: Who needs to coordinate when the backbone changes?
- If they emphasize data: Where does data structure or data lineage become difficult to duplicate?

**Q5. Dual-platform or exit path:** Thinking only about public or anonymizable examples, what would a realistic dual-platform or exit path involve for a large bank?

- If they describe migration effort: Which workstream is hardest: data, process redesign, regulatory reporting, client migration, or internal change?
- If they frame exit as possible: What would a bank need to preserve from day one to keep that option real?
- If they resist the premise: What would be a more accurate way to describe switching difficulty?

#### Block 2 — AUA-Linked Monetization And Incentive Alignment

**Q6. AUA-linked model:** FNZ often presents the AUA-linked model as part of a partnership rather than a standard vendor relationship. What does that pricing structure enable that a fixed-fee model would not?

- If they emphasize alignment: Alignment around what exactly: client growth, asset growth, service quality, product breadth, or long-term investment?
- If they emphasize partnership: What makes it materially different from a vendor contract?
- If they describe risk sharing: Which risks are shared and which remain with the bank?

**Q7. Prioritization:** How, if at all, does that alignment shape day-to-day prioritization decisions?

- If they say it shapes roadmap choices: How do client-specific needs get balanced against reusable platform investment?
- If they say it mostly affects economics: Does it also affect governance conversations?
- If they mention trade-offs: What is the most common source of tension?

**Q8. Tensions:** Where does the AUA-linked model create tension or misunderstanding for clients?

- If they mention cost perception: Do clients compare it to IT cost, operations cost, or revenue participation?
- If they mention control: How do clients think about control when fees scale with assets?
- If they say tensions are limited: What conditions need to be in place for the model to work smoothly?

#### Block 3 — Winner-Take-Most Conditions In Regulated B2B

**Q9. Consolidation:** What combination of factors makes European wealth-platform infrastructure consolidate onto a small number of providers rather than support many parallel platforms?

- If they emphasize regulation: Does regulation favor scale because compliance investment can be reused?
- If they emphasize client demand: Do banks actually want variety in the operating backbone, or mainly reliability and breadth?
- If they emphasize technology: Which platform capabilities become stronger with scale?

**Q10. Relative importance:** Which matters more in practice: switching difficulty, scale benefits, or clients' limited appetite for operational variety?

- If they choose switching difficulty: Where does switching difficulty bite first?
- If they choose scale: What is the clearest scale advantage a client would notice?
- If they choose limited variety: Why is variety less valuable in back-office wealth infrastructure than in customer-facing products?

**Q11. Public mandates:** Looking only at public examples such as Swedbank, UniCredit Germany, or Raymond James Canada, what do those mandates reveal about what clients are buying from FNZ today?

- If they say "transformation": Transformation of technology, operations, governance, or business model?
- If they say "efficiency": What kind of efficiency should the paper distinguish: cost, coordination, regulatory, or scalability?
- If they say "end-to-end": What is analytically important about end-to-end rather than point solution?

#### Closing

**Q12. Five-year view:** If the European wealth-management sector continues on its current trajectory, what does the platform landscape look like in five years?

- If they predict concentration: What should banks or regulators worry about?
- If they predict plurality: What keeps the market from becoming winner-take-most?
- If they mention new entrants: What would a new entrant need to be credible?

**Q13. Open prompt:** Is there anything I have not asked about that is important for understanding FNZ's position in European wealth?

### Questions And Conditional Follow-Ups

#### Opening

**Q1. Role and tenure:** How would you describe your seat at FNZ today, and how has your time at the firm shaped how you read the bank-FNZ relationship?

- If they emphasize longevity: What has changed most over that period?
- If they emphasize operations: Which operating layers do you see most clearly?

**Q2. Boundary framing:** From your seat, where is the boundary between what the bank does and what FNZ does on a typical engagement?

#### Block 1 — Coordination Cost Versus Production Cost

**Q3. Cost changes:** Which costs has technology and platform standardization actually reduced for the banks you work with, and which costs have stayed the same or increased?

- If they mention cost reduction: Is the reduction mainly labor, duplication, reconciliation, coordination, compliance, or change delivery?
- If they mention new costs: Are those transition costs, governance costs, vendor-management costs, or ongoing complexity costs?
- If they resist cost language: What would be a more accurate operational benefit?

**Q4. Conditions for benefits:** Public client announcements often emphasize standardization, governance, and scalability. In operational terms, what has to be true for a bank to experience those benefits?

- If they mention process discipline: What happens when a bank tries to preserve too much legacy variation?
- If they mention data quality: Where does poor data quality hurt the transformation?
- If they mention governance: What governance routines make the relationship work?

**Q5. Evolution:** Over your time at FNZ, how has that cost picture evolved? Where did the shift surprise you?

- If they mention regulation: Has regulation increased the value of shared infrastructure?
- If they mention client expectations: Have banks become more realistic or more demanding?
- If they mention automation: Which human work remains stubbornly hard to automate or standardize?

#### Block 2 — Hybrid Governance Form

**Q6. Governance form:** The bank-FNZ relationship is more involved than a simple vendor contract, but it is not internalized either. How would you describe that governance form in practice?

- If they say partnership: What makes it operationally different from a supplier relationship?
- If they say outsourcing: What remains inside the bank despite outsourcing?
- If they say it depends: Which factors move the relationship closer to partnership versus vendor management?

**Q7. One core partner:** Public cases such as Swedbank suggest banks still evaluate several providers before choosing one primary backbone partner. Why does the operating model so often settle on one core partner rather than several?

- If they mention integration: Which integrations are hardest to split across providers?
- If they mention accountability: Does one backbone simplify incident ownership and regulatory accountability?
- If they mention economies of scale: Are these technology economies, operations economies, or governance economies?

**Q8. Authority line:** Where do you see the line of authority on day-to-day operational decisions, and where does it shift over time?

- If they mention implementation: How does authority differ before go-live and after go-live?
- If they mention incidents: Who decides during incidents or regulatory deadlines?
- If they mention roadmap: How do client priorities enter the platform roadmap?

#### Block 3 — Asset Specificity And Lock-In

**Q9. Specific investments:** What investments does a bank make when it commits to the FNZ platform that become difficult to redeploy elsewhere?

- If they mention data: Is data specificity about format, lineage, controls, reporting, or ownership?
- If they mention people/process: How do internal roles become reorganized around the platform?
- If they mention integration: Which connected systems make switching more difficult?

**Q10. Type of lock-in:** Is the lock-in primarily contractual, technical, operational, data-related, or organizational?

- If they pick one: What is second most important?
- If they say all: Which one does management usually underestimate?
- If they reject "lock-in": What term would better capture the dependency?

**Q11. Underestimation:** What do banks consistently underestimate when they enter a wealth-platform outsourcing arrangement?

- If they mention change management: Which internal stakeholders are hardest to bring along?
- If they mention retained capabilities: What must the bank still be good at?
- If they mention governance: What should be designed before signing, not after go-live?

#### Closing

**Q12. Keep versus outsource:** If you were advising a universal bank today on which functions to keep and which to outsource to a platform like FNZ, where would you draw the line?

- If they distinguish layers: Ask for the cleanest three-layer version of the answer.
- If they focus on risk: Which risks cannot be outsourced even if work is outsourced?
- If they focus on differentiation: Which functions no longer differentiate?

**Q1. Framing:** When a universal bank considers outsourcing wealth-platform operations to a specialist provider, is that usually understood internally as an operating-model change or as a more conventional IT/vendor-outsourcing decision?

- If they say operating-model change: What makes that visible in governance or ownership?
- If they say vendor outsourcing: What risks does that framing create?
- If they say both: Which framing dominates once budgets and implementation start?

**Q2. Retained capabilities:** Which capabilities does the bank believe it must keep or strengthen internally if the operational backbone is outsourced?

- If they mention advisory/client relationship: What does the bank need to invest in to make that real?
- If they mention data: What data rights, skills, or infrastructure matter most?
- If they mention governance: What internal competence cannot be outsourced?

**Q3. Lock-in:** From the buyer side, is lock-in mainly contractual, technical, operational, data-related, or organizational?

- If they pick one: What makes that form of lock-in hardest to manage?
- If they say data: Is the issue access, quality, portability, lineage, or interpretation?
- If they reject lock-in: How does the bank maintain strategic optionality?

**Q4. Concentration and resilience:** How does the bank think about concentration risk and operational resilience when many institutions rely on the same platform infrastructure?

- If they mention regulation: What do supervisors ask for?
- If they mention operational resilience: What tests or contingency plans matter?
- If they say it is manageable: What governance conditions make it manageable?

**Q5. Why now:** Does competitive pressure from tech-forward financial firms such as Revolut, Nordnet, and Avanza materially affect the modernization decision, or are internal cost, regulation, and legacy complexity stronger drivers?

- If they emphasize fintech pressure: Is the pressure on price, speed, user experience, or product breadth?
- If they emphasize internal cost/regulation: Is fintech mostly a narrative used to mobilize change?
- If they say both: Which driver actually wins investment committee approval?

**Q6. Recommendation test:** If a paper recommends that banks outsource standardized operational layers but retain client relationship, advice, brand, distribution, and proprietary data capability, what would you challenge or refine?

- If they agree: Ask what governance mechanisms make the recommendation executable.
- If they challenge it: Ask which retained capability is unrealistic or missing.
- If they add another capability: Ask whether it is differentiating or merely necessary.

## Final Pre-Interview Checklist

- Confirm confidentiality and anonymization.
- Ask permission to record, or confirm that notes-only is preferred.
- Keep the interview to 30 to 45 minutes.
- Start broad, then narrow.
- Ask one question at a time.
- Avoid leading language such as "Would you agree that..."
- Ask "Can you give an example?" whenever the answer becomes abstract.
- Ask "What would make that not true?" whenever the answer strongly supports the thesis.
- End with the open prompt.
- Immediately mark sensitive material that should not enter the report.
