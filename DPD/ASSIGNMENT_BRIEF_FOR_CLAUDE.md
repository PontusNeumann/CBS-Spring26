## 1. The Single Grading Rule

The paper is graded **only** on how well it demonstrates understanding and meets the four Learning Objectives (LOs). Everything else — structure, topic, writing style — is subordinate to the LOs. If a paragraph does not serve an LO, it does not belong in the paper.

### The Four Learning Objectives (verbatim)

1. **Explain** key concepts related to the role of data, digital platforms, and digitalization.
2. **Illustrate** how data, digital platforms, and digitalization influence markets and firms through examples based on business cases or sectoral analyses.
3. **Apply** a set of theoretical tools to analyse the key economic, managerial, and societal impacts of data, digital platforms, and digitalization.
4. **Evaluate** and propose innovative solutions to business problems arising in data, digital platforms, and digitalization, considering the interplay of economic, managerial, and societal factors.

Claude must, at every review pass, score each section against these four LOs and flag sections that serve none of them.

## 2. The Core Task

Find a **real-life** case — a specific firm, a whole industry, or a comparison between two — that contains a genuine business problem, tension, or opportunity tied to the course themes. Then:

- Analyse it using theories, concepts, and models from the course syllabus.
- Reflect on and critique the situation.
- Deliver **concrete recommendations** to the organisation (or industry).

**No theoretical/hypothetical problems.** The case must be rooted in real-world phenomena so that the analysis is grounded in observable evidence.

## 3. What "Applying Theory" Actually Means

This is the single most important distinction in the brief. Claude must enforce it ruthlessly.

### DO NOT

- Summarise theory for its own sake ("Agency theory says X, Y, Z." → dead weight).
- Drop concept names without using them ("network effects, multi-homing, winner-take-all, operating leverage…" strung together is **concept-dropping** and produces shallow analysis).
- Use more theories than needed. Breadth without depth = shallow.

### DO

- **Use** the theory to generate a specific analytical claim about the case. Pattern:
  *"In light of agency theory, the principal-agent properties present in [case] are X. These properties influence [specific behaviour/outcome] in the following way: Y. This implies Z for the firm."*
- Move fluidly between theory and case throughout. Every theoretical claim → tied to the case. Every empirical observation → tied to a theoretical concept.
- If introducing a framework (e.g., "AI Factory has 4 elements: data pipeline, algorithms, experimentation platform, software infrastructure"), **scope explicitly**: "In my analysis I will use only elements X and Y because…" Do not pretend to use all of a framework if you are not.

### How many theories?

**More than one, but not too many.** One theory = insufficient. Five theories dropped in = shallow. Aim for two to three theories/frameworks applied in depth.

## 4. Page Discipline & Structure

- **Maximum 10 pages.** Every page must earn its place.
- **Problem introduction/description: ≤ 1 page.** Keep setup tight.
- **Structure is open** — Claude should pick the structure that best serves the argument, not follow a rigid template.
- A short introductory summary stating focus and scope is useful ("The AI Factory framework has 4 elements; I will use elements A and B because…"). Not mandatory, but often necessary for clarity of scope.
- A standalone theory-summary section is **not required**. Theories should be introduced at the point of use, only as much as needed to apply them.

### Suggested (but not mandatory) structure

1. Introduction + problem statement (≤ 1 page)
2. Brief scope/framework note (where relevant — explain which parts of which frameworks are used)
3. Integrated analysis (theory + case woven together; covers economic, managerial, societal dimensions)
4. Recommendations (concrete, justified, addressing trade-offs)
5. Short conclusion / reflection / limitations

## 5. Audience

The audience is the **examiner/lecturer** — assume they are already knowledgeable in the topic. Do not waste pages explaining basic course concepts in depth. Define terms precisely on first use, then pivot to application.

## 6. Citations — Rules

Citation style must be **homogeneous** throughout. Pick one and stick to it:

- **APA** (standard at CBS), or
- **Footnotes**.

### When a citation is required

- **Any specific numerical claim or fact must be cited.**
  - e.g., "Novo Nordisk's sales forecast declined by X%" → requires citation.
- **Every theoretical claim must cite the originating article/book.**

### When a citation is NOT required

- General statements without numbers.
  - e.g., "AI is changing the pharmaceutical industry" → no citation needed.

### Source hierarchy

- **Syllabus readings take priority.** If a claim is made in a syllabus reading, cite the reading directly — do not cite the lecture slide that references it.
- Lecture slides may be cited **only** when the content is not covered in the syllabus readings.
- External academic articles and credible sources are welcome as supplements, especially for the empirical case.
- Book citations: name, publisher, year of publication.

The paper should use Iansiti & Lakhani as a backbone, supplemented with additional course readings and credible sources where relevant.

### Course theoretical pillars Claude should draw from

- Digital platforms and multi-sided markets (network externalities, platform economics, pricing strategies, governance)
- The role of data and big data in firm decision-making and competitive advantage (data network effects, learning loops)
- Digitalization and its transformation of industries, business models, and value chains
